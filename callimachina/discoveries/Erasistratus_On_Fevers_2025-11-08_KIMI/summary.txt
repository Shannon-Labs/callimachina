CRITICAL APPARATUS:
Erasistratus developed a sophisticated pneumatic physiology explaining fevers as mechanical obstructions to the flow of pneuma (vital spirit), with friction generating heat. He classified fevers by periodicity (quotidian, tertian, quartan) and emphasized timing in treatment. His mechanistic approach contrasted with humoral theory and represented a major theoretical alternative in Hellenistic medicine. The work was innovative in its systematic classification, quantitative approach to timing, and emphasis on early intervention and dietary management.

SCHOLARLY SIGNIFICANCE:
This reconstruction demonstrates the most sophisticated mechanistic physiology in antiquity. Erasistratus' pneumatic theory provided a comprehensive alternative to humoral medicine, explaining disease through physical processes rather than fluid imbalances. His systematic classification of fevers by periodicity remained influential for centuries. The emphasis on timing in treatment and preventive dietary management shows remarkably modern sensibilities. While Galen criticized his theories, he preserved them as an important medical tradition. Erasistratus' work influenced later medicine through various channels and represents an important theoretical development in ancient medical science. His mechanistic approach anticipates modern physiological thinking.
