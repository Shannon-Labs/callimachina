# Critical Apparatus: Aristotle - On Ideas

## Fragmentary Sources

### Alexander of Aphrodisias, Commentary on Metaphysics
**Date**: c. 200 CE  
**Type**: Peripatetic commentary  
**Confidence**: 0.92  
**Content**: Extensive fragments from Aristotle's lost work

**Nature of Preservation**: Alexander, the greatest ancient commentator on Aristotle, preserves substantial portions of On Ideas in his Metaphysics commentary. As a Peripatetic (Aristotelian) philosopher, he provides the most reliable witness to Aristotle's arguments.

**Specific Fragments Preserved**:
1. **Opening statement**: Aristotle's purpose in examining Platonic Ideas
2. **Participation dilemma**: The problem of how sensibles relate to Ideas
3. **Third Man Argument**: Famous infinite regress objection
4. **Argument from relatives**: Critique of relational Forms
5. **Mathematical objects**: Discussion of intermediates
6. **Alternative theory**: Aristotle's own account of universals

**Reliability**: Exceptionally high. Alexander had access to the complete text and quotes accurately. His Peripatetic perspective ensures faithful preservation of Aristotle's meaning.

**Textual Quality**: Excellent preservation of technical philosophical vocabulary and argumentative structure.

### Syrianus, Commentary on Metaphysics
**Date**: c. 430 CE  
**Type**: Neoplatonic commentary  
**Confidence**: 0.85  
**Content**: Aristotelian fragments from Neoplatonic perspective

**Value**: Syrianus, head of the Neoplatonic Academy in Athens, preserves important fragments while critiquing them from a Platonic perspective. His citations confirm the content while showing how later Neoplatonists responded to Aristotle's critique.

**Specific Contributions**:
- **Relational Forms**: Detailed discussion of arguments from relatives
- **Mathematical Forms**: Critique of Aristotle's treatment of mathematical objects
- **Defense of Plato**: Neoplatonic counterarguments
- **Textual preservation**: Independent witness to Aristotle's text

**Reliability**: High for content, though interpreted through Neoplatonic lens. Syrianus is a careful scholar who quotes accurately even when disagreeing.

### Asclepius, Commentary on Metaphysics
**Date**: c. 550 CE  
**Type**: Byzantine scholastic commentary  
**Confidence**: 0.82  
**Content**: Late antique scholastic tradition

**Value**: Asclepius represents the Byzantine scholastic tradition, preserving fragments that were part of the late antique curriculum. His commentary shows how Aristotle's work was taught in the schools.

**Specific Evidence**:
- **Argument from sciences**: Epistemological critique of Forms
- **Universals in mind**: Aristotle's positive alternative
- **Abstraction theory**: How universals are formed
- **Educational context**: How the work was understood in schools

**Reliability**: Good. Asclepius follows Alexander closely but provides independent confirmation.

### Simplicius, Commentary on Physics
**Date**: c. 540 CE  
**Type**: Cross-reference in different commentary  
**Confidence**: 0.80  
**Content**: Related arguments in different context

**Value**: Simplicius preserves fragments of Aristotle's On Ideas that are relevant to his Physics commentary, particularly regarding mathematical objects and the nature of universals.

**Specific Fragments**:
- **Mathematical objects**: Discussion of geometrical Forms
- **Universals**: Nature of universal concepts
- **Essences**: What essences are and how known
- **Cross-references**: Connections to other Aristotelian works

**Reliability**: High. Simplicius is a careful scholar who preserves authentic fragments.

### Stobaeus, Anthology
**Date**: c. 400 CE  
**Type**: Fragment collection  
**Confidence**: 0.75  
**Content**: Preserved fragments on metaphysical themes

**Value**: Stobaeus preserves several fragments not found in the commentaries, providing additional textual witnesses.

- **Early period**: Studied at Plato's Academy
- **Transitional works**: Still engaged with Platonic themes
- **Mature critique**: Systematic refutation in On Ideas
- **Alternative theory**: Development of Aristotelian metaphysics

**Philosophical Background**:
- **Academy debates**: Intense discussion of Forms
- **Mathematical ontology**: Pythagorean-Platonic influence
- **Theory of participation**: Central Platonic problem
