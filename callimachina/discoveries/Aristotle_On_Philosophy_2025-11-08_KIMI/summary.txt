CRITICAL APPARATUS:
On Philosophy was a dialogue (unlike Aristotle's treatises) discussing the nature and value of philosophy. It presents philosophy as the pursuit of wisdom through contemplation of first causes, divided into theoretical (theology, mathematics, natural philosophy) and practical (ethics, politics, economics) branches. Aristotle argues that philosophy begins in wonder, that the gods themselves philosophize, and that the philosophical life is the happiest. The work was influential in Hellenistic and Roman philosophy, providing a model for Cicero's own philosophical dialogues.

SCHOLARLY SIGNIFICANCE:
This reconstruction demonstrates Aristotle's early literary approach to philosophy before his mature technical works. The dialogue form shows his engagement with Plato's methods while developing his own systematic philosophy. The work's influence on Cicero created a bridge between Greek and Roman philosophy, establishing the philosophical dialogue as a genre in Latin. The transmission through Philo and Clement shows how Aristotelian philosophy was adapted in Jewish and Christian contexts, influencing Western thought for centuries. The work represents a key stage in Aristotle's philosophical development and in the transmission of Greek philosophy to the Roman world.
