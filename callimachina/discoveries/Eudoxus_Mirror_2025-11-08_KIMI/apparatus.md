# Critical Apparatus: Eudoxus - Mirror

## Fragmentary Sources

### Aristotle, Metaphysics XII.8
**Date**: c. 350 BCE (Aristotle citing Eudoxus)  
**Type**: Direct citation  
**Confidence**: 0.95  
**Content**: Detailed description of Eudoxus' astronomical system

**Nature of Citation**: Aristotle provides the most extensive ancient account of Eudoxus' system of concentric spheres, describing the number of spheres for each planet and their mathematical relationships.

**Specific Details Preserved**:
1. **Number of spheres**: Sun (3), Moon (3), Each planet (4)
2. **Functions**: Daily motion, zodiacal motion, retrograde motion
3. **Mathematical relationships**: Speed ratios, axis orientations
4. **Observational basis**: How the model explains phenomena

**Reliability**: Exceptionally high. Aristotle is a near-contemporary and provides technical details that suggest direct knowledge of Eudoxus' work.

## Historical Context

### Eudoxus' Career

**Background**: Eudoxus (c. 390-340 BCE) was one of the greatest mathematicians and astronomers of antiquity:
- **Born**: Cnidus (Asia Minor)
- **Studied**: With Plato at Academy, with priests in Egypt
- **Taught**: At Cyzicus, later at Academy
- **Contributions**: Mathematics (proportion theory), astronomy, geography

**Mathematical Achievements**:
- **Theory of proportions**: In Euclid Book V
- **Method of exhaustion**: Precursor to calculus
- **Astronomical observations**: Systematic records
- **Geographical work**: World map

### Astronomical Innovation

**Historical Significance**: Eudoxus' system represents:
- **First mathematical cosmology**: Geometric model of heavens
- **Response to Plato's challenge**: "Save the phenomena" with uniform circular motion
- **Mechanical explanation**: Physical model, not just description
- **Mathematical astronomy**: Precise, quantitative approach

**Influence on Later Astronomy**:
- **Aristotle**: Adopted and modified the system
- **Callippus**: Added more spheres for better fit
- **Aristarchus**: Reacted against geocentric model
- **Ptolemy**: Developed more sophisticated geometric models
- **Copernicus**: Knew Greek astronomical tradition

**Transmission to Arabic Science**:
- **Al-Battani**: Continued mathematical astronomy
- **Ibn al-Haytham**: Developed optical theory
- **Al-Tusi**: Created new geometric models
- **Maragha observatory**: Advanced planetary theory
