CRITICAL APPARATUS:
The Mirror (also called On Speeds) presented Eudoxus' revolutionary system of concentric spheres to explain planetary motion. Each planet required multiple nested spheres rotating at different speeds and axes to produce observed phenomena including retrograde motion. The sun needed 3 spheres, the moon 3, and each planet 4. The system was mathematically sophisticated, using uniform circular motion to "save the phenomena" in accordance with Platonic principles. The work established mathematical astronomy as a discipline and influenced all subsequent ancient astronomy.

SCHOLARLY SIGNIFICANCE:
This reconstruction demonstrates the origin of mathematical astronomy in Greek science. Eudoxus' system was the first geometric model to explain complex planetary motions quantitatively. His methods of nested spheres and speed ratios influenced all subsequent astronomy including Ptolemy's more sophisticated models. The transmission through Aristotle made it the standard cosmological model for centuries. Arabic astronomers developed his mathematical methods further, and the system remained influential until the Renaissance. Eudoxus established the principle that celestial phenomena could be explained through mathematical models, a foundational concept for modern science. His work represents a landmark in applying mathematics to natural phenomena.
