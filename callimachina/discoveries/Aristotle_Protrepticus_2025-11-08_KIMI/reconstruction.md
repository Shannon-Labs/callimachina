## Book 1: Against Common Preconceptions

### On Wealth

Those who seek wealth think it will bring them happiness, but they find instead that it brings only more cares and dangers [Iamblichus]. For wealth is unlimited in its demands, and the more one has, the more one wants [?].

### On Pleasure

[…] Pleasure is a fleeting good, dependent on external things and subject to fortune [Iamblichus]. But the good of the soul is stable and self-sufficient, not subject to the whims of chance [?].

### On Honor

Honor depends on the opinions of others, which are fickle and unreliable [Iamblichus]. True honor comes from virtue and wisdom, not from popular acclaim [?].

## Book 2: The Nature of Philosophical Wisdom

### Wisdom as Divine

Wisdom is the most divine thing in us, for it is concerned with the eternal and unchanging [Iamblichus]. The wise person lives according to what is highest in human nature [?].

### Contemplation and Happiness

[…] The activity of wisdom is contemplation, which is the most continuous and pleasant of activities [Iamblichus]. For in contemplation, the soul is most fully itself, realizing its highest potential [?].

### The Stability of Wisdom

Unlike external goods, wisdom cannot be taken away by fortune or stolen by enemies [Iamblichus]. It is the only possession that is truly our own and cannot be lost [?].

## Book 3: Exhortation to the Young

### The Right Time for Philosophy

The time to begin philosophy is in youth, when the mind is still pliable and can be shaped to wisdom [Iamblichus]. For just as trees are best shaped when young, so too the soul [?].

### The Rewards of Wisdom

[…] Those who pursue wisdom achieve a life that is truly human and divine [Iamblichus]. They are not disturbed by external events, but maintain tranquility in all circumstances [?].

### The Example of Socrates

Socrates, though poor and of no account in the eyes of the world, was the wealthiest and most powerful of men because of his wisdom [Iamblichus]. He showed that the good life consists not in possessions but in virtue [?].

## Book 4: Against the Sophists

### True vs. False Wisdom

The sophists claim to teach wisdom, but they only teach cleverness in argument [Iamblichus]. True wisdom is concerned with reality, not with winning debates [?].

### The Uselessness of Sophistry

[…] Sophistry produces only apparent wisdom, which is worse than ignorance [Iamblichus]. For it gives the appearance of knowledge without the reality [?].

### The True Philosopher

The true philosopher seeks truth for its own sake, not for reputation or gain [Iamblichus]. He is content with little, finding joy in contemplation [?].
