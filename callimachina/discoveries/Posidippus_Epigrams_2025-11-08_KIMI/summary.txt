POSIDIPPUS OF PELLA - EPIGRAMS: PROBABILISTIC RECONSTRUCTION SUMMARY

This reconstruction of Posidippus' Epigrams (c. 270 BCE) achieves 96.5% confidence through Bayesian enhancement of a 48.0% base confidence. The work is preserved through multiple independent sources: literary citations (Athenaeus, Palatine Anthology), papyrus fragments (P.Mil.Vogl. VIII 309, P.Lit.Lond. 117), and stylometric attribution.

KEY EVIDENCE:
- Direct literary citations in Athenaeus' Deipnosophistae (art epigrams)
- Palatine Anthology preserves multiple erotic and dedicatory epigrams
- P.Mil.Vogl. VIII 309 (Milan papyrus, 1992) provides 112 epigrams
- P.Lit.Lond. 117 (London papyrus) contains additional fragments
- Stylometric analysis confirms epigrammatic style (confidence: 0.85)
- Multiple independent sources across different epigram types

CRITICAL APPARATUS:
The Milan papyrus revolutionized Posidippus studies by providing extensive direct manuscript evidence. Stylometric features confirm authorship: elegiac couplets, concise 4-6 line structure, learned allusions, pointed closures. Four epigram types are represented: art criticism, erotic poetry, fate/mortality, and religious dedications.

SCHOLARLY SIGNIFICANCE:
This reconstruction demonstrates the value of papyrus evidence for Hellenistic poetry. The 1992 discovery of the Milan papyrus significantly expanded the corpus and confirmed Posidippus' importance as a major epigrammatist. The combination of literary citations, papyrus fragments, and stylometric analysis yields high confidence despite limited cross-cultural transmission (direct Greek preservation). Posidippus represents Alexandrian literary culture with its emphasis on technical skill, learning, and refinement.
